# Burning Art at Borderland

## Guideline for planning, approving and running safe art burns

This guideline is based on the Talk discussion "Burn Safety: Using Protea as a test case to construct a burn protocol" by Annie Locke Scherer and follow-up comments, 7 June 2024 and later. This is written as an editable guideline for further development, not as final legal or fire-authority advice.

No large artwork may be burned at Borderland unless it has a named Burn Lead, an approved location, a 2-3x height safety radius, marked fire lanes, enough identifiable sober perimeter volunteers, a fire spinning protocol if relevant, a weather/fire-ban backup plan, a go/no-go checklist, and a cleanup/burn-scar plan.

For an overview of how this guideline, burn plans, roles and maintained checklists fit together, see [Working with the guidelines](working-with-the-guidelines.md).

The document has been refined by Erik Lönroth for Borderland 2027.

Peer reviewed 2027 by Tomas Vintilas, Albin Bergstedt.

## Principles

A burn at **Borderland** should never be improvised.

A burning artwork may only happen when the burn has been planned, communicated, staffed, inspected and given a clear go/no-go by the designated burn authority.

The goal is not to stop burning art. The goal is to avoid the unsafe patterns described after previous burns: last-minute placement changes, unclear timing, insufficient exits, fire spinners trapped by audience movement, stray fire props, improvised extinguishing stations and unclear authority.

A good **Borderland** burn is planned early, placed intentionally, communicated clearly, staffed soberly, controlled visibly, and cleaned completely.

## Roles and responsibilities

The following roles are referenced in this guideline and may be relevant to your burn scale and complexity. They help everyone understand how decisions are taken with respect to burn safety, especially for large structures.

For smaller burns, it is sufficient to have a **Burn Lead**.

The same person can hold more than one role, depending on the scale of the burn.

### Role: Borderland Representative

Responsible for communicating with Borderland and authorities.

Checklist coverage: communication responsibilities are represented in [Master operational large burn checklist](master-operational-large-burn-checklist.md).

### Role: Burn Lead

Responsible for the burn plan for the individual burn and has final say in the go/no-go decision. Reports to the Borderland Representative and is also responsible for communication with external entities, such as neighbours and fire departments, unless this is cleared with the Borderland Representative.

The Burn Lead is responsible for the final go/no-go process, but should coordinate with fire safety, Helm/event lead, placement, medical and relevant realities.

Maintained checklists: [Master operational large burn checklist](master-operational-large-burn-checklist.md), [Go/no-go checklist](go-nogo-checklist.md).

The Burn Lead must confirm:

- No active fire ban.
- Weather permits burn.
- Wind direction acceptable.
- Wind speed acceptable.
- Perimeter established.
- Fire lanes clear.
- Perimeter crew present and sober.
- Extinguishing plan ready.
- Fire spinning completed or safely separated.
- Fueling/ignition controlled.
- Audience informed.
- Emergency access open.
- Cleanup plan ready.

The Burn Lead has authority to say: **No Go** That decision must be respected without debate at the moment of decision.

### Role: Safety Lead

Responsible for the safety review and reports to the Burn Lead.

Maintained checklist: [Master operational large burn checklist](master-operational-large-burn-checklist.md).

The Safety Lead has authority to say: **No go.** That decision must be respected without debate at the moment of decision.

### Role: Medics Lead

Has no responsibility within the burn itself, but should be informed about the burn and should know who the Burn Lead is.

Reports to the Borderland Representative.

Maintained checklist: [Master operational large burn checklist](master-operational-large-burn-checklist.md).

### Role: Volunteer / Perimeter Crew Lead

Responsible for the volunteer/perimeter crew.

Reports to the Burn Lead.

Maintained checklists: [Volunteer training checklist](voulonteer-training-checklist.md), [Audience information checklist](audience-information-checklist.md), [Releasing the perimeter checklist](releaseing-the-perimiter-checklist.md).

### Role: Fire Artists / Fire Spinner Lead

Responsible for artists that perform with fire within the fire perimeter. This role is specific to burns and has special considerations covered in later chapters.

Has the authority to decline artists from participation as a fire artist within the burn perimeter.

Reports to the Burn Lead.

Maintained checklist: [Fire Artists / Fire Spinner Lead checklist](fire-art-spinner-lead-checklist.md).

### Role: Ignition Team

Responsible for preparing and executing ignition only after the Burn Lead has given a clear go.

Reports to the Burn Lead.

Maintained checklist: [Ignition crew checklist](ignition-crew-checklist.md).

### Role: LNT Lead

Responsible for the LNT plan.

Reports to the Burn Lead.

Maintained checklists: [Master operational large burn checklist](master-operational-large-burn-checklist.md), [Fire Artists / Fire Spinner Lead checklist](fire-art-spinner-lead-checklist.md#post-burn-cleanup--lnt).

## Burn categories

These categories help you understand to what degree you need to prepare and take actions to perform a safe and beautiful burn at Borderland.

### Small contained fire

Examples: fire bowls, small campfire-like contained flames, braziers.

Requires ordinary fire safety rules, extinguishing equipment and permission according to current site rules.

### Fire performance / fire spinning

Examples: poi, staffs, fans, dragon staff, dipping stations, performer fire.

Requires separate fire spinning protocol, safety briefing, fuel handling, extinguishing stations and performer coordination.

### Intentional burn

Examples: effigy, temple, tower, sculptural build designed to burn.

Contained by dimensions less than a 5 m x 5 m x 5 m volume.

Requires a written burn plan and approval.

### Large public burn

Examples: Protea-scale burn, large effigy, tall structure, symbolic central burn, or anything expected to attract most of **Borderland**.

Requires a full burn plan, named Burn Lead, perimeter crew, fire lanes, fire safety review and a documented go/no-go process.

2. Backup timeline and hour in case of "No go", rain, etc.
3. LNT timeline.

## Backup plans

For fire ban conditions or a "No go", the backup plan is one of:

- Postponed: burn later, possibly during autumn/winter.
- Non-fire burn: lights, sound, projection, or programmed "blinky burn".
- Dismantle and remove instead of burning.

The burn should not depend on "we'll decide Saturday night." That is exactly the kind of last-minute situation this guideline is meant to prevent.

## Burn safety

This chapter covers burn safety items.

### Fire burn perimeter

For large burns, the burn perimeter should be approximately 2-3x the height of the structure.

This is the minimum inner audience safety radius, not a complete ember/forest safety calculation.

**Example**

The structure is 7.5 m tall.

Using the 3x rule: 7.5 m x 3 = 22.5 m.

So the safe perimeter radius was 22 m from the burn.

This radius protects people from heat, collapse and immediate burn danger. It does not automatically prove that the forest is safe from flying embers. Forest distance, wind, drought, material choice and fire department input must be handled separately.

### Fire lanes and exits

Large burns must have emergency corridors for people to be able to exit.

Minimum: 2 fire lanes, each at least 2 m wide.

Recommended for accessibility and crowd flow: 4 fire lanes, one in each direction where possible. The idea is that no-one gets trapped between a burning structure and an exit.

Fire lanes must be:

- Marked and/or trivially apparent before the burn.
- Kept clear during the burn.
- Known to perimeter volunteers.
- At least one lane connected to emergency access routes, such as fire truck or ambulance access.
- Emergency access routes must not be blocked by spectators, bikes, art, carts, bars, camps or sound systems.

A perimeter without exits is not safe.

### Volunteers / perimeter volunteers

For large burns, quite a few people are needed to help with various tasks. One of those tasks is acting as a perimeter volunteer.

Volunteers are tasked with supporting the burn and art team in making the burn beautiful and, at the same time, providing safety and comfort during the drama that occurs when large structures burn.

### Recruiting volunteers

As a recommendation, recruit roughly double the number actually required.

Why over-recruit?

- People fail to show up.
- Some may not be sober.
- Volunteers can work in pairs.
- One person can handle a situation while the other maintains overview.
- Extra people can act as runners, for example catching people that try to run into the fire.
- Pairing makes volunteering more social and less lonely.

### Pre-burn training of volunteers

For large burns, all perimeter volunteers should get access to a training session about:

Use [Volunteer training checklist](voulonteer-training-checklist.md) as a good starting point for the Volunteer / Perimeter Crew Lead when preparing the volunteer training.

- Who is who.
- What happens when.
- How to act if there is an accident, where fire lanes are, etc.
- Basic safety training.
- Ideally, how to extinguish a burning person.
- That they have authority to act to ensure the safety of the burn.
- That they should be sober enough to make safety decisions.
- Feeling part of making the burn enjoyable for everyone. This is important.

### Perimeter volunteers during the burn

Perimeter volunteers should:

- Be spaced approximately 5 m apart. This is an approximation.
- Try to identify people trying to breach the perimeter as early as possible and stop that from happening.
- Feel comfortable and safe at all times.
- Not do anything heroic.
- Have something that makes them easy to identify, for example FAST vests or another highly visible marker.
- Be visibly different from ordinary participants, performers and artists so the audience knows who has authority.

Good advices for Fire Guardians / perimeter volunteers:

- Once everything begins, it may be impossible to hear. Establish visual signals to communicate transitions to the team.
- For other communication, walk up to a guardian. That guardian can then walk to the next guardian until the message has been communicated to all.
- Do not station a Fire Guardian on the downwind side due to the possibility of flying embers.
- Station Fire Guardians close to water buckets.
- Guardians face the crowd and smile. They are inviting people to participate in the artist's work, not only enforcing a boundary.
- Fire Guardians should keep an eye out for lonely or nervous people in the crowd, then encourage the crowd to take care of them.
- When the Safety Lead gives Guardians the signal, they can start moving the buckets closer to the fire, indicating that runners can start soon.
- Once the line has been dropped, Fire Guardians who have not been given an ongoing job are released.

### Fire spinning / fire artists safety

Fire spinning must be treated as a separate safety reality from the burn.

Past problems identified which can be included in the information to the fire spinning crew:

- Fire spinners surprised by naked/running audience members.
- Performers trapped between audience and effigy.
- Safety personnel separated from performers.
- Dipping stations placed downwind of the burn.
- Embers landing near fuel containers or dipped props.
- Improvised extinguishing stations.
- Random people arriving with props without safety briefing.

#### Required fire spinning protocol

The Fire Artists / Fire Spinner Lead is responsible for executing this protocol.

Fire spinning should preferably happen before the burn, not simultaneously. This avoids the audience becoming overexcited around active performers and a burning structure at the same time.

Use [Fire Artists / Fire Spinner Lead checklist](fire-art-spinner-lead-checklist.md) as the maintained checklist for the needed training and instructions. Keeping the actionable checklist in that file avoids double work when the protocol changes.

A Fire Artists / Fire Spinner Lead must ensure:

- All performers registered in advance.
- Safety briefing completed.
- Proper fuel provided.
- Dipping etiquette explained.
- Dipping stations placed safely relative to wind.
- Dipping stations protected from embers.
- Extinguishing stations marked.
- Fire blankets / towels / extinguishers available.
- Fire spinning safety crew on shift.
- Random unbriefed performers excluded.
- Authority to intervene is clear.

People who did not attend the safety briefing should not participate in fire spinning.

## Materials guideline for burns

Different materials produce different burn aspects: flame height, burn speed, smoke, smell, ember behavior, collapse behavior, heat intensity and cleanup complexity.

Use [Materials guideline for burns](materials-guideline-for-burns.md) as the maintained material inventory and reference for preferred and avoided materials.

The art team needs to consider the materials as part of the artwork design and burn plan. The Burn Lead needs to make sure that the selected material is consistent with the overall safety plan for the burn, including perimeter size, forest risk, extinguishing plan, cleanup plan and any fire department communication.

## Forest and ember risk

The audience perimeter and the forest safety distance are separate questions.

A 22 m audience radius for a 7.5 m structure may protect participants from heat and collapse, but it does not automatically answer:

- Can embers reach the forest?
- Can glowing particles travel in the wind?
- Can a forest fire start later from hidden embers?
- What wind speed is acceptable?
- What distance to forest is acceptable?
- What drought conditions are acceptable?

There is no numeric answer for safe forest distance, but for a significantly large structure, such as a large public burn, at least 50 m should be kept clear to forests. This assumes a low amount of embers and low wind, less than 10 m/s with no gusts.

Therefore, for hill burns or burns near trees, large distance to forest should be prioritized until expert guidance exists.

For large burns near forest, **Borderland** should consult or inform the local fire department and/or qualified fire safety experts.

## Hill burns

A burn on the hill is not automatically forbidden, but it requires extra scrutiny and additions to the burn plan.

Hill-specific considerations:

- Hill size.
- Crowd capacity.
- Proximity to forest.
- Flying sparks.
- Neighbor visibility.
- Fire department calls.
- Access to water.
- Fire lanes.
- Past burn scars.

### Hill burn requirements

A proposed hill burn must include in the burn plan:

- [ ] Map of burn radius.
- [ ] Map of audience area.
- [ ] Map of fire lanes.
- [ ] Map of forest distance.
- [ ] Map of emergency access.
- [ ] Wind direction assumptions.
- [ ] Crowd capacity estimate.
- [ ] Neighbor visibility plan.
- [ ] Water / pump plan.
- [ ] Burn scar prevention plan.

Use 3 people per square meter as a rough crowd-density planning formula to test whether the hill could fit **Borderland** with the required safety radius. That may be useful for rough planning, but it should not replace crowd safety review.

## Neighbor visibility plan

Visible burns may cause neighbors to report a fire in good faith. **Borderland** should therefore consider the perspective of someone nearby seeing flames and wondering whether the site is in danger.

For larger category burns, the plan should address in a **Fire department communication plan**:

- Should neighbors be informed?
- Which neighbors?
- Should the fire department be informed?
- Is a permit required?
- Should fire department presence be requested?
- Who is the contact person if someone calls?
- Can the fire department phone **Borderland** before dispatching?
- What information reassures them that the burn is controlled?

## Fire department communication plan

It is always good to know how to communicate with the fire department prior to a burn. This can typically be coordinated with the Borderland Representative, but nevertheless, it is good to make sure there is a communication plan for the fire department.

For larger burns and burns on hills, this is mandatory.

## Water and extinguishing plan

Large burns must have a realistic extinguishing plan. Extinguising in this context doesn't mean that the burn should be able to stop a forest fire, its purpose is to be able to prevent the fire from spreading uncontrollably and mitigate smaller fires that may arise.

Possible resources:

- Extinguishers.
- Water tanks.
- Buckets.
- Hoses.
- Engine-driven pump.
- Water from lake, if feasible.
- Fire blankets.
- Rakes and shovels.
- Marked extinguishing stations.
- Trained people assigned to use them.

Extinguishing equipment must be placed before ignition and must remain accessible throughout the burn.

## Burn scars and ground protection

**Borderland** already has multiple burn scars. Future burns should not casually create new permanent damage.

Every large burn must include a burn scar prevention and restoration plan.

Questions to answer:

- Will this reuse an existing burn scar?
- Will the ground be protected?
- Will aggregate, stone, gravel or other protection be used?
- Can the protection material be removed or reused?
- How will ash be collected?
- How will nails, screws, metal and debris be removed?
- Who signs off the cleanup?

The Protea thread notes that burn scar prevention may require meaningful preparation, potentially digging down and adding stone or low-fine aggregate, with restoration afterward.

This section needs further local expert input before becoming a final technical standard.

## Audience communication

Before the burn, participants should be told:

- When the burn is happening.
- Where to stand.
- Where not to stand.
- Where the exits are.
- Who the safety volunteers are.
- That fire lanes must stay clear.
- That the burn may be cancelled.
- That crossing the perimeter may stop the burn.
- When fire spinning happens.
- When the burn area is safe to enter afterward.

Suggested announcement:

> This burn will only happen if the Burn Lead and Safety Lead say conditions are safe. Stay behind the marked perimeter. Keep fire lanes clear. Follow instructions from people in FAST vests. Do not enter the burn area until it is officially released.

## Ignition

Ignition may only be done by the designated ignition crew. This is typically the art crew, as they normally like to be in control over that process, but they must comply with the items for a safe burn. The Burn Lead is responsible for making sure those considerations have been followed.

Use [Ignition crew checklist](ignition-crew-checklist.md) as the maintained checklist for ignition preparation and go/no-go readiness.

Rules:

- No random participant ignition.
- No spontaneous fuel throwing.
- No unapproved accelerants.
- No ignition before perimeter is complete.
- No ignition while people are inside the hot zone.
- No ignition while fire lanes are blocked.
- No ignition if weather has changed.
- No ignition if the Burn Lead has not given all-clear.

Fueling, if any, must happen only after the initial perimeter is established and only under the approved plan.

**Important:** Do not under any circumstances arm the structure, such as adding flammables, at any point before a "Go" is approved by the Burn Lead.

### During the burn

During the burn, the safety team / lead watches:

- Wind shifts.
- Ember travel.
- Crowd movement.
- Perimeter breaches.
- People trying to run or dance into the hot zone.
- Fire spinners or props entering unsafe areas.
- Collapse behavior.
- Secondary ignitions.
- Smoke direction.
- Medical issues.
- Forest risk.

The perimeter may be expanded. The burn may be suppressed. The burn may be stopped from progressing if safety conditions change.

### Releasing the perimeter

The perimeter is not released when the flames look pretty.

The perimeter is released only when the Burn Lead or Safety Lead confirms:

Use [Releasing the perimeter checklist](releaseing-the-perimiter-checklist.md) as the maintained checklist for deciding when the perimeter can be released.

- Structure has collapsed or stabilized.
- No unsafe leaning material remains.
- Hot spots are controlled.
- No active flying ember risk.
- Fire lanes are no longer needed.
- Fire spinning equipment is clear.
- Extinguishing crew agrees.
- Area is safe for limited entry.

Participants should not be allowed to walk into hot ash or unstable debris.

This information is best communicated during the volunteer training session.

### Cleanup / LNT

The artist/crew remains responsible after the ritual moment ends.

Use the [Fire Artists / Fire Spinner Lead checklist](fire-art-spinner-lead-checklist.md#post-burn-cleanup--lnt) as the maintained post-burn cleanup / LNT checklist for the art crew.

Artist cleanup must include:

- Ash removal.
- Nail and screw removal.
- Metal debris removal.
- Wire/staple removal.
- Unburned material removal.
- Ground inspection.
- Daylight MOOP sweep.
- Burn scar inspection.
- Restoration if needed.

A magnet rake or equivalent should be used where metal fasteners were present.

The burn is not complete until the site is clean.
